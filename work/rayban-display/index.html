The opportunity

02 / 16

Staying present, yet connected.

Ray-Ban Meta gave people a camera and a voice. But anything visual still sent them back to their phone, and every glance down is a small exit from the room.

Meta Ray-Ban Display answered with a glanceable, private screen, so you could check in without reaching for your phone. And for early adopters, the pull of a genuinely new kind of device.

The bet

03 / 16

Meta had to break new ground and keep it premium.

A glanceable in-lens display paired with a novel gesture interface, marketed as a premium upgrade to the Ray-Ban Meta.

The constraints

05 / 16

Split attention, all-new input.

Glanceable. Worn in motion, every screen could only borrow a sliver of attention, never demand it.

Multimodal input. No touchscreen, a new vocabulary of Neural Band gestures, a temple touch bar, and voice, with no cursor to see.

Coherent. Each app had to feel like one system, so the platform felt familiar fast.

Meta Ray-Ban DisplayZachary Halvorson

How I worked

06 / 16

Prototyping until the interaction felt right.

Prototype the feel. A spec can't tell you if a gesture feels good. I built working prototypes against the real hardware.

Tune by hand. Tuned timing, thresholds, and motion until each interaction disappeared.

Hold the bar. This rigor let me move across surfaces and ship each at quality.

Craft artifact · 02

11 / 16

A teleprompter only the wearer can see.

Teleprompter had a hard deadline with readability and control problems blocking ship. I drove the in-glass UI and mobile companion through launch. The key call came from research: we tested auto-scroll, but speakers preferred to control when each line appeared, so I shipped manual cards driven by the Neural Band.
