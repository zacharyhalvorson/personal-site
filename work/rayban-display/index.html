Problem framing

02 / 12

Premium hardware is only desirable if it feels effortless.

Meta Ray-Ban Display paired a private, glanceable screen in your eyeline with the Neural Band, a wristband that reads the smallest hand gestures. A premium, first-of-its-kind device.

But novelty alone wouldn't earn a place on someone's face. Every interaction had to feel so easy and natural that the glasses became something people genuinely wanted to wear.

The constraints

04 / 12

Designing for split attention, in the flow of real life.

Display glasses are worn in motion, while you are walking, cooking, mid-conversation. Every screen could only ever borrow a sliver of attention, never demand it, so the bar was to inform the moment without overloading it.

01

Glanceable

A small, monocular display you take in at a glance. Every screen had to earn its pixels, show only what matters right now, and then get out of the way of the world.

02

Gesture-native

Input comes from subtle finger movements on the Neural Band, with no cursor to see. Controls had to be learnable and forgiving.

03

Coherent

Each app had to feel like one system, not a set of one-offs, so the whole platform felt familiar fast.

How I worked

05 / 12

Prototyping until the interaction felt right.

01

Prototype the feel

A spec cannot tell you whether a gesture feels good. I built working prototypes against the real display and Neural Band to find out.

02

Tune by hand

I iterated the timing, thresholds, and motion of each interaction until it felt effortless inside tight constraints.

03

Hold the bar

That prototyping habit let me move quickly across many surfaces and ship each one at the same quality bar.

Music, in the corner of your eye.

I owned the Music app end to end, from OS integration to player UI, playlist suggestions, and voice experience. Craft artifact · 02

08 / 12

Write a message with your fingertip.

Messaging was a hero feature for the display, and handwriting was how we showcased the Neural Band, a wearables-native, novel input method. I designed the autocomplete UX in messaging and the handoff between writing by hand and picking a suggestion, so a quick reply stays one fluid motion.

Craft artifact · 03

09 / 12

A teleprompter only the wearer can see.

Teleprompter had to balance information density with readability, with controls obvious enough to trust in a high-stakes moment on camera. I owned the in-glass display UI and the mobile companion UX through launch, resolving the readability and control problems that were blocking ship.
