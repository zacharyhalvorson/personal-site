What changed

03 / 22

New silicon turned a quick clip into a sustained point of view.

Ray‑Ban Stories could already capture short clips, but thermal and battery limits made continuous streaming a non‑starter.

The Ray‑Ban Meta processor changed the equation. For the first time the glasses could hold a continuous, hands‑free point of view, untethered, long enough to actually be live.

The opportunity

04 / 22

Why we bet the launch on livestreaming.

Why now

Post‑pandemic, POV video was surging, GoPro action sports, immersive travel walks, and a wave of first‑person cooking from creators like Kenji López‑Alt and Susi Vidal. Research showed the content was laborious to make; creation was the bottleneck on how much could exist. The glasses could remove it.

Why streaming won

Of the features explored, streaming integrated most seamlessly across the Family of Apps. Livestreamers are a small cohort, but they post aspirational content with outsized reach, and tap‑to‑buy attribution could turn those impressions into sales.

What success looked like

05 / 22

One new processor, two ways to win.

Wearables carried two mandates: sales and retention. The new processor's streaming let one capability answer both: Leveraging livestreamer's outsized reach to drive the sale, and video calling to earn the repeat usage.

Sales & marketing driver

Livestreaming

A splashy launch moment, aspirational POV like a race‑car driver's view to headline Meta Connect, paired with tap‑to‑buy attribution that turns a creator's reach into device sales.

Retention driver

POV video calling

User research kept surfacing one want above all — to feel present with the family and friends they love. POV video calling answered it directly: the intimate, everyday experience of sharing what you see, the repeat use that keeps the glasses on your face.

System design · failure states

14 / 22

Designing for a dropped stream.

A dropped stream needed to degrade as gracefully as possible. Utilizing push notifications, audio, and LED cues to let the user know how to restart their broadcast was key to an experience users could trust.

Ray‑Ban MetaZachary Halvorson

System design · sharing

15 / 22

Communicating all the ways a stream can drop.

A live stream rides a fragile chain, from glasses to phone to network to app. I mapped the full system of warning and error states so it always degrades gracefully instead of going dark.

Interactive matrix of fail modes

Audio and hardware interaction

16 / 22

Going live handsfree.

The ability to stream handsfree meant the whole broadcast could run through sound and touch. A tap on the glasses arm plays back the most recent comment, announcements for when the first viewer joins and the last one leaves, and soft earcons mark reactions as they land, so a creator always had a read on the room.

Long tap to check in

Ray‑Ban MetaZachary Halvorson

Shipping at quality

17 / 22

Internal livestream talent show.

To hold a high quality bar, I ran a weekly livestream “talent show.” Teammates volunteered to go live from the glasses while the org tuned in over lunch. Dogfooding became a ritual, and rough edges surfaced long before launch.

It is also how we tuned the audience engagement experience, hearing viewers’ reactions read aloud mid‑stream until they landed naturally in the moment.

Outcome · attribution

19 / 22

Every stream became a storefront.

A creator going live from the glasses carried an attribution badge into the stream, so viewers could tap straight from an aspirational POV moment to buy the glasses themselves. Content attribution 10x'd our sales expectations.

Landing it meant partnering closely with Instagram aligning on a unified glasses icon and content design, then shepherding it through their reviews so the badge read as native to IG while still unmistakably Ray‑Ban Meta.
