What changed

03 / 20

New silicon turned a quick clip into a sustained point of view.

The previous generation, Ray-Ban Stories, could already capture short clips, but thermal and battery limits made continuous streaming a non-starter.

The Ray-Ban Meta processor changed the equation. For the first time the glasses could hold a continuous, hands-free point of view, untethered, long enough to actually be live.

The opportunity

04 / 20

Why we bet the launch on livestreaming.

Why now

Post-pandemic, POV video was surging, GoPro action sports, immersive travel walks, and a wave of first-person cooking from creators like Kenji López-Alt and Owen Han. Research showed the content was laborious to make; creation was the bottleneck on how much could exist. The glasses could remove it.

Why streaming won

Of the capture modes in flight, streaming integrated most seamlessly across the Family of Apps. Livestreamers are a small audience, but they post aspirational content with outsized reach, and tap-to-buy attribution could turn those views into glasses sales.

What success looked like

05 / 20

One new processor, two ways to win.

Wearables carried two mandates: sales and retention. The new processor's streaming let one capability answer both: Livestreaming to drive the sale, video calling to earn the return.

Sales & marketing driver

Livestreaming

A splashy launch moment, aspirational POV like a race-car driver's view to headline Meta Connect, paired with tap-to-buy attribution that turns a creator's reach into device sales.

Retention driver

POV video calling

The intimate, everyday experience of sharing what you see with the people you love, the repeat use that keeps the glasses on your face.

System design · sharing

13 / 20

Designing for every way a live stream can drop.

A live stream rides a fragile chain, from glasses to phone to network to app. I mapped the full system of warning and error states so it always degrades gracefully instead of going dark.

Shipping at quality

15 / 20

Making dogfooding fun

Internal livestream talent show.

To hold a high quality bar, I ran a weekly livestream “talent show.” Teammates volunteered to go live from the glasses while the org tuned in over lunch. Dogfooding became a ritual, and rough edges surfaced long before launch.

It is also how we tuned the audience engagement experience, hearing viewers’ reactions read aloud mid-stream until they landed naturally in the moment.

Craft artifact · 03

17 / 20

Tap-to-buy on a live stream

Every stream became a storefront.

A creator going live from the glasses carried an attribution badge into the stream, so viewers could tap straight from an aspirational POV moment to buy the glasses themselves.

Landing it meant partnering closely with Instagram — co-designing a unified glasses icon and the in-stream content design, then shepherding it through their reviews so the badge read as native to IG while still unmistakably Ray-Ban Meta.
